No Way We're Gay (The Song of the Very Straight Men)

lyr. Miriam Axel-Lute, mel. "Away, Away With Rum"

We never wear kilts, because kilts are like drag,
And one little swish turns a man to a fag.
Can you imagine a deeper despair…
Than a man who went gay 'cause he wanted some air?

No way, no way we’re gay. No way! No way we’re gay. No way we’re gay. No way, no way we’re gay. No way! And we’ll tell whoever will listen.

We never eat tofu, 'cause tofu has soy,
Full of estrogens that can make girls out of boys.
And can you imagine a deeper defeat…
Than a man gone all femme 'cause he wouldn’t eat meat?

We won’t let gays marry, and it is no wonder,
For every gay marriage tears a straight one asunder.
Your dear mother, wouldn’t she wail and moan…
If her daughter’s gay neighbors made her husband leave home?

We never wear pink, 'cause pink is too prissy,
And even one stripe turns a man to a sissy.
Can you imagine a sorrier sight…
Than a man with no balls 'cause his red was too light?

Our sons don’t play dress-up, 'cause dress-up’s confusing,
And it doesn’t teach them 'bout winning and losing.
Can you imagine a deeper disgrace…
Than your son playing sports with some rouge on his face?

Our sons don’t have dolls, because dolls are like babies,
And boys who like babies are gay, there’s no maybes.
Imagine a guy who can’t tell who to fuck…
'Cause his folks let him play with things other than trucks!

Notes: Miriam when asked about the story/program notes:

There’s not a ton to say about it really – it was one of those that came all at once in half an hour kind of things. A funny thing, though I’m not entirely sure it’s for public consumption, is that the person who inspired it doesn’t know that, and makes a big show of asking for it so often I had to start turning him down. (It’s not a direct description of anything he does.)

Other funny story was singing it at Pinewoods in a room with a lot of Scottish dancers… I was advised to switch the order so I didn’t start with the kilts verse and cause terrible mayhem. There was still a big indrawn breath, but at least they knew what I was about by then.

(Based on Song of the Salvation Army; I learned from Julia Rosenfeld.)
